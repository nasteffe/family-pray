Oceania

Tiny islands sprinkled across the world's biggest ocean, so far apart that between them lies nothing but blue for days of sailing. Some are low rings of coral barely higher than the waves; some are green peaks. The people here were the greatest sea-explorers in history, finding these specks of land across a vast ocean long ago.

How Life Works Here

The sea is everything: people fish, gather coconuts, and share what the reef and the palm give. Because their waters are so full of tuna, the islands let bigger countries' boats fish there in exchange for money that helps run their schools and clinics. One tiny country, Tuvalu, even earns money by lending out its internet name — ".tv"!

What Is Happening

Here is the hardest and most important thing: the sea is slowly rising, because the whole world is warming, and on the lowest islands the water is creeping closer to people's homes and gardens, and salt is spoiling their wells. This is frightening, and it is not their fault — these islands burned almost none of the fuel that warms the world. But the island people are not just waiting: they are leaders, standing up at the world's big meetings to say "our homes matter," building sea walls, saving their languages and songs, and asking every other country to burn less. Their courage speaks for the whole planet.

Something Wonderful

Long before maps or compasses, the people here sailed thousands of miles across the open Pacific in canoes — steering by the stars, the shape of the waves, and the flight of birds — and found tiny islands no bigger than a village. They carried this wayfinding knowledge in memory and song, and today young islanders are learning to sail the old way again.
