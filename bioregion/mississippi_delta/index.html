Mississippi Delta

Mexico, United States

Nearctic

A mighty river — the Mississippi — gathers water from half of North America and slows into a maze of marshes, bayous, and islands where it meets the warm Gulf. Herons stalk the shallows, shrimp boats chug out at dawn, and music seems to grow here like the moss on the trees.

How Life Works Here

The river is a highway: barges carry corn and grain from farms far upstream down to the sea. Fishing families catch shrimp and oysters. And along the riverbank stand rows of huge factories and refineries, turning oil into fuel and plastic for the whole country.

What Is Happening

The communities living closest to those factories — many of them Black families whose great-great-grandparents were enslaved on this same land — breathe dirtier air than almost anyone else in the country, and more of them get sick. The wetlands that protect everyone from storms are washing away too. But the neighbours are not quiet: grandmothers and pastors have formed groups that stand up in meetings and courts to say our children's air matters, and they have already stopped new factories from being built.

Something Wonderful

The Mississippi Delta gave the world a whole new kind of music — the blues, which grew into jazz, rock and roll, and much of what you hear today. Out of hard times, people made something so beautiful the whole world sings it.

Clean Water, Dirty Water

Put some soil in a cup of water and stir it up. Look how dirty it is! Now pour it through a cloth into another cup. The cloth catches the dirt. Many families do not have a way to clean their water. Think about how lucky we are to turn on a tap.

You will need:
  • two cups
  • soil
  • cloth or coffee filter
