Zambezi Plateau

Afrotropic

A high, wide land of miombo woodland in southern Africa, where the great Zambezi river gathers itself and then — at one astonishing place — falls off the edge of the world. Elephants cross old paths, and baobabs stand like grey giants.

How Life Works Here

Farmers grow maize and cassava; fishers work the great lakes and the Indian Ocean shore. Under the ground lie treasures — rubies, and gas beneath the sea floor — that big companies from far away have come to take.

What Is Happening

In northern Mozambique, armed men attacked towns and villages; people have been killed, and many families fled with only what they could carry. It began, in part, near the places where the gas and rubies are — treasure nearby did not become blessing for the people living there. Cyclones from the warm ocean have also smashed homes. But neighbours rebuild together, churches and mosques shelter families side by side, and farmers replant after every storm.

Something Wonderful

The local name for Victoria Falls is Mosi-oa-Tunya — "the smoke that thunders." The falling water throws up a cloud you can see from far away, and on bright full-moon nights it makes a moonbow: a rainbow at night.

Let's Do Something Together

Soil in Your Hands

Go outside and pick up a handful of soil. Look closely — can you see tiny pieces of leaves, roots, or small creatures? Good soil is alive! Families who farm need good soil to grow food. When the land is tired or dry, nothing will grow and families go hungry.

You will need:
  • access to outdoor soil or a pot of earth
  • magnifying glass (optional)
For grown-ups

Soil degradation from overgrazing, deforestation, and monoculture reduces yields year on year. In conflict zones, agricultural land is often deliberately destroyed or mined, removing it from production for years.
