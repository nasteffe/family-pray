Tethys Gulf

Palearctic

A hot desert shore along a warm gulf, where in one lifetime small pearl-diving towns have grown into cities of glittering glass towers, the tallest in the world. Date palms shade old oases, and out on the water, people once dived deep for pearls holding only their breath.

How Life Works Here

Deep beneath the sand and sea lies oil and gas — enormous amounts — which ships carry away to power much of the world and which built the shining cities. Almost all the building and much of the daily work is done by people who came from other countries — India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, the Philippines — sending their wages home to their families far away.

What Is Happening

This is a rich and peaceful place, and it is worth asking an honest question about it: the towers were built by workers who are far from their children and are not always treated fairly or paid enough for such hard work in such heat. It is right to notice that the comfort of some rests on the work of others. But people are pushing for better — new rules for workers' rest, pay, and safety — and the workers themselves send home money that builds schools and homes in distant villages.

Something Wonderful

Before the oil, this whole coast lived from the sea: divers went down again and again on a single breath to find pearls, guided by songs sung on the boats. In one person's lifetime, a village of pearl divers became a city with the tallest building on Earth. Few places have changed so fast.
