Palearctic

People call this place the Fertile Crescent — a green curve of rivers and fields between deserts, where some of the very first farms, cities, and alphabets in the world began. The rivers Tigris and Euphrates have watered it for longer than history can remember.

How Life Works Here

Farmers here grow wheat for bread, just as they did thousands of years ago. Shepherds keep sheep on the hills. Under the ground there is oil, which ships carry across the sea to run cars and factories far away.

What Is Happening

There has been war in parts of this place for many years — in Syria, in Gaza, in Iraq before that. People have been killed, homes have been broken, and millions of families are living away from home, some in tents. The rivers are also lower than they used to be, so the wheat harvests are smaller. But bakers still bake, teachers still teach in tent schools, and neighbours share bread — in this place, sharing bread with a guest is one of the oldest rules of all.

Something Wonderful

Bread itself may have been invented here! People were baking flat loaves in this land more than ten thousand years ago. Every sandwich you eat is a little bit of the Fertile Crescent's story.

Let's Do Something Together

The Water Cup Game

Fill a cup with water. Now pour half into another cup — that is for washing. Pour half of what is left into a third cup — that is for cooking. What is left is for drinking. When water is scarce, families must choose how to share what little they have.

You will need:
  • three cups
  • water
For grown-ups

Water scarcity forces impossible choices. When aquifer levels drop or rivers are diverted, families reduce hygiene and food preparation before cutting drinking water — each choice carries health costs.
