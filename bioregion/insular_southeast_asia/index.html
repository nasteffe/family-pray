Indomalaya

Not one land but seventeen thousand islands, scattered across the warmest seas on Earth like a handful of green jewels. Volcanoes smoke gently, and beneath the waves lies the coral triangle — home to more kinds of sea creatures than anywhere else in the ocean.

How Life Works Here

The sea is the road here: ferries and fishing boats stitch the islands together. On land, oil palms grow in long rows — their oil ends up in biscuits, soap, and shampoo all over the world — and under some islands lies nickel, a metal needed for electric-car batteries.

What Is Happening

The palm rows and the nickel mines keep growing, and the forest people and the fishing villages are the ones who pay: forests that belonged to Dayak communities are cleared, and mine-mud clouds the reefs where the Bajo people fish. But islanders are standing up for their waters — Indigenous communities are winning back forest rights, and young people are replanting mangroves along the shores.

Something Wonderful

The Bajo people are sometimes called sea nomads: they live on and over the water, children swim almost before they walk, and scientists found that many Bajo can hold their breath and dive deeper than almost any other people on Earth. The sea is truly their home.
