Australasia

A whole continent of its own — red desert in the middle, green rainforest in the north, and a coast where a reef of living coral stretches longer than any other on Earth. The First Peoples of Australia, the Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples, have cared for this land for tens of thousands of years — the oldest living cultures anywhere.

How Life Works Here

Under the red earth lies coal and iron, dug up in enormous mines and shipped to make steel and electricity in countries far away. Farmers raise sheep and grow wheat on huge dry stations, and along the coast people live beside that famous warm sea.

What Is Happening

This is a peaceful, lucky country, with two real care questions. One is the reef: the sea is growing warmer and the coral, which is alive, turns pale and can die — so scientists and divers are racing to protect and regrow it. The other is fairness: much of the mining happens on land that belongs to Aboriginal peoples, whose voice was long ignored, and who are working to be properly heard about their own country.

Something Wonderful

The Aboriginal peoples have kept their stories, songs, and knowledge of the land alive for more than sixty thousand years — the longest unbroken cultures on the whole planet. And the Great Barrier Reef is so vast and alive it can be seen from space, built by tiny creatures smaller than your fingernail.
