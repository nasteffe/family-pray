Indomalaya

Two great rivers — the Mekong and the Irrawaddy — run from the mountains down through green Southeast Asia to the sea. Their valleys are stitched with rice fields that shine like mirrors when they flood, and golden temple spires rise above the trees.

How Life Works Here

Rice is life here: families plant it, temples bless it, everyone eats it. The rivers give fish — millions of people's dinners swim in the Mekong. In the far north of Myanmar, people dig for jade, a green stone more precious than gold in China.

What Is Happening

In Myanmar, soldiers took over the country's government, and many people who said "no" to them have been killed or have fled — families are hiding in forests and camps. The jade mines make a few powerful people rich while the diggers stay poor and in danger. And new dams on the Mekong are changing the river that feeds so many. But people keep helping: monks shelter the displaced, and villages along the rivers share their fish and rice.

Something Wonderful

Once a year in Cambodia, a river actually runs backwards! The Tonle Sap river reverses its flow when the Mekong floods, filling a huge lake with fish. People hold a festival to celebrate the river's turning.

For grown-ups
