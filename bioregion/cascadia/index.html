Cascadia

United States, Canada

Nearctic

A green corner of North America where snow-topped volcanoes look down on forests so wet and mossy they are called rainforests — even though it's cool, not hot. Rivers rush down to a cold grey sea where orcas hunt in families, and the trees grow taller than almost anywhere on Earth.

How Life Works Here

For thousands of years the first peoples of this coast have lived by the salmon — fish that are born in the rivers, swim out to the ocean, and then fight their way all the way back upstream to lay their eggs. Whole cultures grew up around welcoming them home each year. People also cut the great timber and grow apples and berries in the valleys.

What Is Happening

This is a mostly peaceful, green place. The care question here is about the salmon: dams built across the rivers make it hard for the fish to get home, and fewer come back each year. The first peoples who have always depended on them are leading the work to help — and in some rivers, old dams are being taken down so the salmon can run free again, and they are coming back.

Something Wonderful

Salmon can find their way home from the wide ocean to the exact stream where they hatched — some think they smell it. And when they die after laying their eggs, their bodies feed the whole forest: scientists have found ocean fish inside the tallest trees. The salmon feed the forest that shades the salmon.

Let's Do Something Together

The Water Cup Game

Fill a cup with water. Now pour half into another cup — that is for washing. Pour half of what is left into a third cup — that is for cooking. What is left is for drinking. When water is scarce, families must choose how to share what little they have.

You will need:
  • three cups
  • water
For grown-ups

Water scarcity forces impossible choices. When aquifer levels drop or rivers are diverted, families reduce hygiene and food preparation before cutting drinking water — each choice carries health costs.
