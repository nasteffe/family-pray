Central America

Mexico, El Salvador, Nicaragua and 5 more countries

Neotropic

A narrow green bridge of land between two oceans, strung with volcanoes like a dragon's back. Rain forests hum with howler monkeys, and in the cloud forests lives the quetzal — a green bird with a tail like a ribbon, which ancient peoples thought too beautiful to cage.

How Life Works Here

On the volcano slopes, families grow some of the world's best coffee. Many families also live in two places at once: a mother or father works far away in the north and sends money home every month — that money is love in an envelope, and it builds houses and pays for school shoes.

What Is Happening

Life is hard in some countries here: jobs are few, storms and dry spells hurt the harvests, and in some cities gangs make neighbourhoods unsafe — so many families set out on the long road north, which is dangerous, hoping for safer work. Being apart hurts. But shelters along the road give travellers beds and soup, and the money sent home keeps grandmothers and children going.

Something Wonderful

Walk uphill on one side of this land and rain flows to the Atlantic; a few steps over the ridge, it flows to the Pacific. There are places where a child can stand with one foot in each ocean's watershed.

Where Does Our Food Come From?

Look at something you ate today. Where did it grow? If you have a map, try to find the country. Food travels a very long way to reach our plates! In some places, families grow their own food, but when the rains do not come, the crops cannot grow.

For grown-ups

Global supply chains connect consumer choices to land use in crisis regions. Cash crop exports (coffee, cocoa, palm oil) often displace subsistence farming, making communities dependent on volatile markets.
