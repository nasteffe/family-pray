Indomalaya

This is the great river plain of South Asia, where the Ganges, the Indus, and the Brahmaputra come down from the highest mountains on Earth and spread out to feed more people than almost anywhere else. Every summer the monsoon arrives — weeks of warm rain the whole land waits for.

How Life Works Here

Rice and wheat grow in the river water; everything moves — rickshaws, trains, boats. And millions of people, many of them young women, sew the world's clothes in huge factories. Check your T-shirt's label: "Made in Bangladesh" means hands here stitched it.

What Is Happening

The rivers that give life also flood, and the floods are getting bigger as the climate changes — every year families in the delta lose their homes to the water and must start again. The people who sew our clothes often work very long days for very little. But this plain never gives up: flood shelters open before the water comes, neighbours pull each other's rice to dry ground, and the garment workers are joining together to ask for fair pay.

Something Wonderful

The water in the Ganges begins as snow on mountains so high that aeroplanes fly lower. A single drop can travel from a glacier, through a farmer's rice field, past a city of millions, to the sea — and rise again as monsoon rain. People here greet the rivers like elders.

Let's Do Something Together

Soil in Your Hands

Go outside and pick up a handful of soil. Look closely — can you see tiny pieces of leaves, roots, or small creatures? Good soil is alive! Families who farm need good soil to grow food. When the land is tired or dry, nothing will grow and families go hungry.

You will need:
  • access to outdoor soil or a pot of earth
  • magnifying glass (optional)
For grown-ups

Soil degradation from overgrazing, deforestation, and monoculture reduces yields year on year. In conflict zones, agricultural land is often deliberately destroyed or mined, removing it from production for years.
